## Tweet 8 — Quant: correlation

One thing I obsess over as a quant: how correlated are my positions?

Most retail portfolios run 0.40-0.60 average correlation. When the market drops, everything drops together. That's not diversification — that's an illusion.

My portfolio right now: 0.151 average correlation across 11 positions. Zero pairs above 0.60. That translates to 51.4% risk reduction just from how the positions interact.

The biggest position ($EDEN.PA, 18.7% of portfolio) has a correlation of ~0.05 with everything else. It basically moves on its own. That's not luck — it's by design.

During a crisis, correlations spike. Everyone sells everything. But starting at 0.15 instead of 0.50 means even when correlations double, you're still in better shape than most.

Real diversification isn't owning 50 stocks. It's owning 11 that don't move together.

## Tweet 10 — Reverse DCF

Here's a trick I use constantly: instead of predicting what a company is worth, I flip it around. Take the current stock price and calculate what growth rate the market is assuming.

$GDDY at $81. The market is pricing in -10.9% annual FCF decline for 5 years. The company has actually been growing FCF at +19.7% per year. That's a 30-point gap.

$ADBE at $255. Market assumes -7% annual FCF decline. Actual growth: +10%. Revenue up 12% last quarter. AI ARR tripled.

The market is literally pricing these as dying businesses. The numbers say the opposite.

Doesn't mean the market is wrong forever. But when the gap is this big, usually somebody is making a mistake. And historically, buying when the implied growth is deeply negative has worked out pretty well.
